What You're Good At

rarely easy

I used to think that being good at art was a trait, like being tall. This was before I had really talked to a lot of artists about how they were good at art. I got the simplest answer back - it was because they liked it.

That was a code-word for they drew/painted/sculpted/did art a lot. I mean like hours every day. And what separated the great artists from the good artists was that the great artists spent twice as much truly focused time on art. I still think that what separates the famous great artists from great artists is equal parts fantastic luck and knowing the right people.

The idea of the genius who can parse even the most complex material in one go, is a myth. Well, there are probably a couple, but with that kind of extreme innate ability comes an extreme innate cost. Hard work, which produces the same kind of result but for a different reason, costs precious time, and for more extreme things, possibly physical/mental health.

My current impression of all this is that it comes down to a kind of inner toolbox. Those who make it seem effortless have the right tool for the job. After all, if you try to tighten a nut with a hammer, it’s not going to work. Few are born with all the tools, although they can definitely be born with a good feel for some of them (read: talent). Some people have a lot of tools but aren’t proficient in many of them. Some people only have a couple of tools but are really really proficient in them. I would suppose that the former describes the curious person who knows a lot but doesn’t actually try it out. The latter is the kid who aced all the tests in high school and claimed they were easy, and then gets crushed like a soda can in college when his tools stop working and he doesn’t know how to build new ones.

I’ve yet to find some subject in which talent wins over hard work.
